CarlSwap

Project idea developed by Lucky Beulla Muhoza, Kritika Pandit, Owen Xu and Daniel Scheider as part of the Human Computer Interactions class.
Carleton College © March 2025

Problem statement

There isn’t an efficient system for Carleton students to exchange items, both during the school year and for the end of the year cleanup. Our project aims to provide an interface for students to offer up extra items they no longer need to other students during the term, as well as provide a better way for the Center for Community and Civic Engagement (CCCE) office to collect and redistribute items acquired during the end of term cleanup.

Low-fidelity prototype

Our prototype addresses key user needs identified during structured interviews. One participant suggested incorporating a rental feature for textbooks and other returnable items, which we have included as an option in the platform. Another concern was the accumulation of unwanted items throughout the term, leading to a rush to donate at the semester’s end. To address this, the prototype features a Student Shop for selling and donating items throughout the term and a CCCE Shop, which becomes active at the start and end of the semester to redistribute items collected during year-end cleanups. Additionally, a messaging feature has been introduced to help users coordinate pickup and drop-off arrangements efficiently.

Team’s UX research process

Our team conducted semi-structured interviews and fictional inquiry with main stakeholders, including Carleton students and the CCCE office.

User-driven design: Two participants sketched their vision of the platform and proposed features they wanted after we introduced our project idea. These rough sketches provided valuable insights into user expectations and needs.

Interviews and feedback: Other participants shared their challenges with the current item exchange system and suggested improvements through structured interview protocols. This helped us understand pain points and opportunities for enhancement.

The insights gathered from these sessions directly informed our requirements and shaped the design of our mid-fidelity prototypes.

Team’s analysis process

After conducting our research, we transcribed the audio recordings and created an axial codebook to analyze the data.

Axial coding: We categorized the transcripts into key themes, including phenomenon, conditions, action/interaction strategies, and consequences to identify patterns in user feedback.

Double coding and consensus: Each transcript was coded by two teammates, ensuring accuracy and minimizing bias. We then compared codes, reached a consensus on overlapping insights, and refined our themes.

Requirement prioritization: Using the finalized codes, we extracted the most critical user needs and translated them into prioritized design requirements for our prototype.

Final team requirements (top 3)

Limit storage: Implement a messaging feature that allows users to coordinate the pickup of purchased items, eliminating the need for a designated storage space.

Item postings:

  • Wishlist and notification: Enable a wishlist and notification system so students receive updates when an item they need is posted, reducing the need for constant manual checks.
  • Homepage updates: Display the most recent listings at the top of the homepage, ensuring users can easily find newly available items.

Item tracking: Automatically update an item’s availability status in real-time to reflect whether it has been sold or is still available.

Interaction flow description

  • Login page to Homepage: After registering or logging in with their Carleton username, users are directed to the homepage.
  • Homepage to Wishlist page: Users can access their wishlist by clicking the heart icon located in the bottom navigation bar.
  • Back to Homepage: Users can easily return to the homepage from any page within the app.
  • Item detail page: Clicking on an item from the homepage takes the user to a page with more detailed information about the listed item.
  • Message icon to Messaging feature: Users can message the seller directly by clicking the messaging icon on the item listing.

How the prototype addresses the final requirements

Messaging Feature: Each listed item includes a built-in messaging option, allowing buyers to contact the seller directly within the app. This facilitates inquiries about the item and enables buyers and sellers to coordinate pickup details after payment, eliminating the need for designated storage.

Wishlist and Availability Updates: Users can save items to a wishlist, where unavailable items are automatically tagged. If an item becomes available again, it is updated accordingly, keeping buyers informed without requiring constant manual checks.

Homepage Updates: The homepage defaults to displaying newly listed items first, ensuring users always see the latest available options. Additionally, once an item is sold, a "Sold Out" tag is automatically applied before the listing disappears from the platform, keeping availability status accurate.

UX evaluation process

Method used: Co-Discovery

Two participants worked together to complete tasks while thinking aloud. They discussed their thoughts and actions with each other, providing insights into how they were navigating the interface, identifying challenges, and interpreting design elements. This collaborative approach helped us uncover usability issues and areas for improvement based on these user interactions.

Participants:

  • Freshman – Has not gone through the move-out process but has needed to acquire or get rid of items affordably.
  • Sophomore – Has participated in end-of-year move-out, donated to Carleton pods, and acquired items through CCCE donation events.

We were unable to gather feedback from one of our major stakeholders, the CCCE office, due to the timing of the evaluation (a busy period in the academic term). Since the CCCE manages donated items on campus and facilitates exchanges between students, their input would have been valuable. Consequently, the evaluation primarily reflected the student stakeholder perspective.

UX evaluation tasks

  • Task 1: Finding an item
  • Task 2: Viewing item details
  • Task 3: Adding an item to wishlist

UX metrics used

  • Task completion time: Measured using a phone timer or screen recording from task initiation to completion.
  • Navigation efficiency: Assessed through screen recordings of users performing searches, filtering items, or selecting listings.
  • Error rate: Observed and recorded user errors, such as misclicks or difficulty navigating.

UX metrics collected/results

The UX evaluation revealed that users could quickly find items from the homepage (3 seconds) and intuitively add them to their wishlist (2 seconds). However, viewing item details took longer (12 seconds) as some users hesitated before clicking the item card.

A key issue observed was misclicking on text instead of icons for essential actions, such as messaging the seller. To improve usability, adding a visual cue (e.g., an icon or button) was suggested to make item details more accessible.

Overall, navigation was efficient, with minor adjustments needed for clarity and user interaction.

Prototype improvements based on UX evaluation

1. Messaging background contrast

User feedback: "The messaging page background is too bright. The gradient blue background from the original page was better."

Improvement: Adjusted the messaging background color to reduce brightness and improve contrast.

2. Displaying listings horizontally vs vertically

User feedback: "Maybe you can make it so you can see multiple items at once, rather than just one. Right now, I wish I could just scroll down instead of swiping left."

Improvement: Changed the item layout to add a vertical display instead of only requiring horizontal swiping on the homepage.

3. Visibility of the login button

User feedback: "On the first page, I think adding a rim around the login button would make it more visible and aesthetically pleasing."

Improvement: Enhanced the login button visibility by adding a white border.

Future steps

Modifying the Figma prototype to display the designs of the following functionalities and their specific interactions:

  • Payment options: Support for PayPal, Venmo, credit cards, and Cash App.
  • Adding to cart: Currently, buttons are present but non-functional (no page displaying cart items).
  • Personal details page: Enables users to list items and add them to the platform.
  • Order & listings history: Allows users to track their selling and purchasing activity.
  • Profile customization: Users should be able to update personal information such as username and location on campus for privacy and accuracy.
  • Clickable icons and text: Adjust interaction design to ensure both text and icons are clickable, enhancing usability. Users tended to click on text instead of the icon for key actions.

The evaluation process was relatively easy but felt incomplete because not all Minimum Viable Product (MVP) features were implemented in the mid-fidelity prototype. It lacked full functionality and only met three prioritized user requirements determined after the UX research process. Future evaluations should be conducted once all core functionalities are incorporated into the prototype.

These enhancements would provide better results from our UX evaluation process since they align with user expectations and thus would significantly improve the usability testing of the platform.
